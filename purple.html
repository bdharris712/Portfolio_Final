Two Purple Case Studies



Mattress Differentiation

The Problem

  • Customers struggled to determine which of all the mattress models listed on-site was best for them. This is an industry-wide problem, especially in the internet mattress-in-a-box category and post-COVID, when most shopping takes place online.
  • Poor product fit meant high rates of product returns, which are costly both financially and in customer satisfaction. Not all customers accept an exchange, so we lose directly to competitors when the mattress isn't right the first time.
  • Numerous interviews revealed that the process of shopping for a new mattress, regardless of brand, is often unfamiliar (typically 10+ years since last mattress purchase) and frustrating to navigate. Our website was part of the problem.

The Old Category Landing Page (CLP)

  • Cutaway images are attractive, but ultimately don't teach the customer enough about mattress feel.


  • Product descriptions do not help consumers decide on a specific product


  • Prices are deceiving; those listed are for Twin size


  • Direct side-by-side comparisons require an additional click/page


The Old "Compare All Mattresses" Page

  • This page has a very long scroll.


  • Phrases like “all body types” don't offer a way for customers to “disqualify” bad fits; reducing the number of options to hold in mind is crucial.


  • Descriptions are too generic–they don't really help distinguish between models.
  • After scrolling, users can no longer see the names of the models they are comparing.


  • Quotes don't directly link the features of a specific model to its unique benefits.


  • Users aren't familiar with terms like “No-Pressure Support.” All brands use jargon like this, so it's hard to know what's real vs. marketing.
  • The long scroll continues, dominated by verbose text.


  • For certain model comparisons, all the text is duplicated. Any differences are hard to spot.


  • Most people don't get this far down the page.


  • Coils are a major difference in product feel; the descriptions here don't reflect that. This is too technical.


Research

  • User Interviews: Shop-alongs on actual websites

  • Surveys: Traits of own beds and desired traits in a new one

  • In-Person "Flop Tests": Ratings of actual models on various scales (support, softness, responsiveness, etc.)

  • My Role: Research lead for web design testing, interviews, and surveys; moderated 1/3 of in-person tests.

New CLP, Version 1

  • The CLP now includes direct model comparisons without any additional clicks.

  • Product descriptions focus on tangible features related to the unique feel of each model

  • Bar graphs provide a quick visual gauge to replace wordy descriptions

  • Pricing reflects Queen size, resulting in a “more premium” aesthetic

New CLP, Version 2

  • Each collection (Original and Hybrid) now includes a description to differentiate it from the other

  • Product descriptions are even more focused on feel

  • Bar charts are slimmer, and model feel is explicitly listed in the table

  • Product reviews are linked above product descriptions
  • Rather than relying on the DTC platform to deliver the right product on its own, we explicitly offer two ways to “try before you buy” to ensure satisfaction.

  • The upper image quickly earned trust with consumers, even if they never shopped in-store.

  • The lower animation demonstrates the technology; low-cost samples make a large purchase safer.

Outcomes

  • Usability test participants can explore mattress options and select one that meets their needs faster than before the refresh. They also rate their confidence in their decision higher than before.

  • Test participants rate the brand as more premium and luxury than before.

  • Future updates to the mattress pages, including new imagery and renderings, will continue rolling out over the next quarter. TBD if return rates will decline through 2022. We continue to monitor listening posts for customer feedback about the shopping process.





Advertsing User Testing

The Problem

  • Acquisitions wanted to test certain top-performing ads to determine why they performed well with certain audiences.
  • eCommerce wanted to know how to better handle the user's transition from paid media to a web session.
  • This selection of videos focuses on two distinct messages around Purple's proprietary GelFlex Grid technology: instant adaptiveness and cool temperatures.
  • Videos were prepared in 15-second and 30-second cuts.

Sinking Feeling


Naturally Cool


Research (solo project)

  • Moderated one-on-one interviews, avg. 30 minutes each
  • Selection criteria: Currently in the market for a mattress above a certain price point
  • Questions focused on brand perceptions, product quality, message comprehension and recall, and trustworthiness of claims

Comprehension

The main idea of the videos was sometimes quite difficult for participants to identify and articulate.

At times, the Purple delivery man is difficult to understand due to enunciation and aspirated consonants, including when he uses the words "instantly adapts," which is central to message comprehension. One interview participant watched the clip several times and still could not understand the speech.

The text overlays at the end of the video explicitly describe the main point of each video, but viewers didn't catch the meaining because the video itself was answering a much more important question for them—"How does it feel?"


This animation plays for only 2 seconds, but was unanimously considered the most helpful part of the entire video. People had to understand what the product is before they could even begin to understand how it would benefit them. Nothing in the video up to this point had addressed the more fundamental question in users' minds. Most users did not even recall seeing any on-screen text in the video.

Shorter videos were sometimes easier for test participants to comprehend. They were also proportionally more "pro-Purple" and less "anti-memory foam" than the longer cuts, another plus for comprehending the benefits of the brand.

Everyone recognized Purple as the sponsor of the advertisement.

Most of each video is focused on memory foam, not Purple's technology.

Everyone enjoyed the lighthearted and fun tone of the advertisements.

Not everyone appreciated the "confrontational vibe" of the Purple delivery man character.

Cooling vs. Adaptiveness

In general, the "sinkhole" or "black hole" of memory foam is a bigger pain point than sleeping hot, though both videos were generally convincing. Prior bad experiences with memory foam mattresses were very relatable while watching the videos.

The main point of the cooling videos was more easily discerned.

The "coolness factor" of a mattress company seemed to be a non-starter for most viewers, who did not see a mattress company as a lifestyle brand.

Outcomes

  • My team demonstrated the need for better testing prior to finalizing creative and launching media campaigns. We have since contracted with an agency to perform more basic testing and to track ad performance more effectively.
  • Insights gained around the use of imagery and information needs have been implemented across the organization.
